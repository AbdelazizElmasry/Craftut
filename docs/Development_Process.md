# Development Process

## Timeline

**Development duration:** Approximately 10–12 months from initial concept through exhibition-ready MVP.

**Major phases:**

1. Initial concept
2. Early prototype
3. Design refinement
4. MVP development
5. Public demonstration
6. Post-demonstration improvements

**Current status:** The project is undergoing design revisions and improvements based on lessons learned and identified limitations.

---

## Version 1 Prototype

A first prototype existed before the MVP.

**Purpose:**

- Validate core concepts
- Explore feasibility
- Reduce technical uncertainty
- Collect development insights
- Inform future architecture decisions

This version was primarily an engineering learning platform rather than a customer-facing product. It enabled the team to test motion system behavior, enclosure concepts, and control strategies before committing to the MVP architecture.

## MVP Version

The second version became the exhibition MVP.

**Major improvements over Version 1:**

- Refined industrial design
- Strong visual identity
- Space-inspired aesthetic language
- Large 7-inch touchscreen
- Colorful space-themed UI
- Functional and decorative lighting
- Improved customer-facing appearance
- Focus on accessibility and usability

The product was intentionally designed to feel approachable and engaging rather than resembling a traditional industrial 3D printer. This was a deliberate product identity decision to reduce intimidation for first-time users.

## Team Structure

The project involved:

| Role | Contribution |
|------|--------------|
| Product Designer | Industrial design, visual identity, CMF |
| Mechanical Engineer | Motion system, structure, enclosure mechanics |
| Embedded Engineer | Firmware, controller integration, electronics |
| UI/UX Designer | Interface design, interaction model, visual system |
| Branding Team | Brand identity, visual language, exhibition materials |
| Software Team | Website and mobile application (future platform) |

The Product Development Lead coordinated activities across disciplines and supported integration between teams. This included resolving conflicts between mechanical constraints and industrial design intent, aligning firmware capabilities with UX requirements, and ensuring safety architecture was respected across all domains.

## My Role

**Role:** Product Development Lead / Technical Lead

**Responsibilities:**

- Product vision definition
- Requirements definition
- System architecture decisions
- CoreXY architecture selection
- Controller architecture decisions
- Safety architecture definition
- Product integration
- Mechanical design participation
- Electronics architecture participation
- User experience definition
- System debugging
- Cross-functional coordination
- Exhibition preparation
- Stakeholder communication

**Distinction between leadership and direct contribution:**

As Product Development Lead, I owned architecture decisions, integration strategy, and cross-functional alignment. I participated directly in mechanical design discussions and electronics architecture planning, but the detailed mechanical engineering and embedded firmware implementation were executed by dedicated specialists. My technical contributions were primarily at the system level: defining interfaces, establishing constraints, debugging integration issues, and validating that subsystem decisions aligned with product-level requirements.

---

## Development Approach

The team operated with a bias toward rapid learning and visible progress. Early prototypes were built to test assumptions rather than optimize performance. The MVP was scoped to demonstrate product-market fit and collect user feedback at a public exhibition, not to serve as a production-ready unit.

This approach enabled validation of industrial design and user experience concepts, but it also meant that certain technical parameters—particularly structural rigidity and high-speed performance—were not fully optimized before the exhibition.
